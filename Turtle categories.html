Green Turtle

The green sea turtle (Chelonia mydas), sometimes referred to as the green turtle, black (sea) turtle, or Pacific green turtle, is a species of giant sea turtle that belongs to the Cheloniidae family. The genus Chelonia only contains one species of it. With two different populations in the Atlantic and Pacific Oceans, as well as the Indian Ocean, its range encompasses all tropical and subtropical oceans worldwide. The common name alludes to the typically green fat underneath the carapace rather than the olive to black color of the carapace.

olive ridley

The olive ridley sea turtle, which reaches a maximum length of 61 cm (as measured along the curve), is named for its olive-colored, rounded, heart-shaped carapace. Both sexes reach the same size as adults, however females have a little more rounded carapace than males. Loggerhead

loggerhead sea turtle adults typically reach a length of 1 m (3 ft) and a weight of 80 kg (180 lb). A hawksbill was once captured that weighed 127 kg (280 lb). The turtle's shell, or carapace, has an uneven pattern of light and dark streaks on an amber background, with mainly black and mottled-brown colors spreading off to the sides. The loggerhead sea turtle is unique among sea turtle species in a number of ways. Its tall, tapering head terminates in a mouth that resembles a beak; unlike other beaks, this one is more sharply defined and hooked. The forelimbs of the hawksbill feature two discernible claws on each flipper.
